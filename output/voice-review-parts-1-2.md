## 1. Connor's Voice

### Overall Assessment: STRONG (8/10)

Connor's voice is the backbone of this novel, and across all six sampled chapters it is remarkably consistent in its essentials: the breezy, self-congratulatory internal narration; the performative confidence; the Australian slang deployed as identity; and the widening gap between his Instagram persona and his inner reality.

### Australian Slang

The slang usage is well-calibrated and consistent:

- **"Legend"** -- appears in Ch 1 (three times: pen-lending father, WiFi password, Felix greeting), Ch 5 (none in dialogue, but implicit), Ch 9 ("I'm a legend"), Ch 13 (none), Ch 17 (caption: "KL, you absolute legend"). Frequency is appropriate. It does not feel overused.
- **"She'll be right"** -- appears in Ch 1 ("She'll be right... Just need to get to the hostel") and Ch 5 (twice, including the crucial doubling: "'She'll be right,' Connor thought. Then, less certainly: 'She'll be right.'"). The doubling in Ch 5 is excellent -- it transforms a catchphrase into a moment of psychological fracture. Does not appear in any of the Part 2 chapters reviewed, which is actually a problem (see Fixes section below).
- **"Sick"** -- Ch 1 ("Fully sick"), Ch 5 ("sounds sick"), Ch 9 ("this is sick"), Ch 17 ("It's pretty sick"). Consistent deployment. The "this is sick" reaction to Razak's death map in Ch 9 is a standout moment of dark comedy -- Connor using the same word for a death archive that he uses for a sunset.
- **"Mate"** -- appears throughout all chapters. Properly blankets his dialogue. Note: he calls Felix "my mate" in rationalisations (Ch 9, Ch 13), which is excellent -- the word becomes a weapon of self-deception.
- **"No worries"** -- Ch 1 only (to the woman on the plane). Could appear once more in Part 2 for completeness.
- **"Reckon"** -- Ch 13 ("Best city in Southeast Asia, reckon"). Sparse but correct. This is a word Connor would use without thinking.
- **"Fully"** -- Ch 1 only ("Fully sick"). Could use one more appearance.

**Minor gap:** The catchphrases "Relax, it's Asia" and "That's what travel insurance is for" (from VICTIM_PROFILE.md) do not appear in any of the six sampled chapters. These are listed as defining catchphrases and should appear at least once each in Parts 1-2.

### The "Machine" Metaphor

This is the novel's central psychological device, and its deployment across Parts 1-2 is carefully managed:

- **Ch 9 (first explicit appearance):** "The rationalisation was smooth and fast and complete, a machine of self-deception so well-oiled it ran without friction." This is the establishment passage. It works. The word "machine" does the heavy lifting, and the associated vocabulary (oiled, friction, gears) is introduced cleanly.
- **Ch 13 (escalation):** "The machine in his head was running at full speed" / "the machine intercepted it and rerouted" / "the machine recovered. The gears caught. The production resumed." Three appearances in one chapter. This is the right density for the chapter where Connor hears the David story -- the machine has to work harder because the threat is more explicit. The "gears caught" variation is strong.
- **Ch 17 (rest-day variation):** "the machine ran hardest, because the machine had nothing to process except itself" / "the machine substituted the familiar calculation." Two appearances. The reflexive variation (machine processing itself) is clever and shows escalation -- the machine is becoming parasitic.
- **Ch 20 (breakdown):** "The machine tried to run. He could feel it trying -- the gears engaging, the mechanism reaching for raw material" / "The machine ran and produced silence." Three appearances. The machine's failure in this chapter is the structural payoff of its establishment in Ch 9. The "produced silence" inversion is the strongest single use of the metaphor in the sample.

**Assessment:** The machine metaphor is introduced at the right time (Ch 9, not Ch 1), escalated appropriately, and given a satisfying near-failure in Ch 20. It does not appear to be overused -- no chapter has more than three explicit mentions, and the metaphor is varied rather than repeated verbatim. The progression from "well-oiled" (Ch 9) to "ran at full speed" (Ch 13) to "running past its tolerances" (Ch 20) to "produced silence" (Ch 20) shows genuine arc.

**One concern:** The metaphor is absent from Ch 1 and Ch 5 entirely. This is the correct choice for Ch 1 (the machine hasn't been engaged yet; Connor is still in pre-danger mode). But Ch 5 is the chapter where Connor first seriously considers Felix's offer and where the rationalisation pattern begins. A very early, proto-machine moment here -- not the full metaphor, but a hint of the mental mechanics -- would strengthen the connection. The LinkedIn passage (lines 51-55) where Connor starts a thought-leadership post and can't finish it is a missed opportunity: the self-deception is already operating, just not named yet. Consider adding a brief interior moment, something like: *The thought stalled, and something smooth and practised replaced it -- not an argument, exactly, but a redirection, the mental equivalent of changing the channel.*

### Performative Instagram Persona vs. Inner Doubt

This duality is one of the novel's great strengths, and it is handled with escalating precision:

- **Ch 1:** Connor films himself, narrates his stories ("This is what travel is, guys"), and the Instagram persona is essentially seamless -- there is no visible gap yet. The gap is implied by authorial framing (the phone at 4%, the rounding-up of followers) but not by Connor's own awareness.
- **Ch 5:** The gap opens. The LinkedIn passage is devastating: "He didn't know what you needed. He didn't know if he'd built anything meaningful." The flat white photography sequence is perfectly observed -- the quarter-turn of the cup, the filter, the lie. And the follower count audit ("Some of them were bots, and some of them were his mum's friends") is darkly comic. The chapter's last line -- "The smile didn't reach his eyes. But nobody was looking at his eyes. Nobody ever was." -- is the best single-line summary of the duality in the sample.
- **Ch 9:** "He was seeing content. He was seeing posts. He was seeing a thread." Connor viewing the death map as Instagram material is the duality at its most revolting. This is the character's voice operating at maximum self-indictment.
- **Ch 17:** The bank-balance-vs-Instagram-likes structural contrast is the duality rendered in pure form: "The likes were the smallest number he cared about and the largest number anyone would see. The bank balance was the largest number he cared about and the number no one would see." Excellent. Though see note in Fixes about a slight overstatement in the passage.
- **Ch 20:** The duality collapses. Connor is no longer posting. He is sitting in a chair staring at a wall. The Instagram persona has been silenced by the truth of Wei's back room. This is the correct trajectory.

### Voice Break: One Instance

**Ch 20, lines 121-122:** "...the mechanical prerequisite for the moment when everything would move at once and the landscape would change and the man who had been carrying packages would become the man who had carried packages, past tense, the tense of consequences."

This is beautiful prose, but it is not Connor's voice. This is the narrator stepping in with an essayistic observation about verb tense. Connor does not think in grammatical terms. The "past tense, the tense of consequences" construction belongs to a literary essayist, not to a man having a breakdown in a hostel common room. In a third-person-limited POV, this line pushes against the boundary of what the narrative consciousness should articulate.

**Suggested fix:** Cut "past tense, the tense of consequences." Let the sentence end at "the man who had carried packages" -- the shift from present participle to past participle is already doing the work without the gloss.

## 2. Felix Decker's Voice

### Overall Assessment: STRONG (8.5/10)

Felix is a harder voice to sustain because he must simultaneously charm the reader and unsettle them. The balance is achieved primarily through absence -- Felix's voice is never given POV, so we see him only through Connor's perception, which means his menace is refracted through Connor's denial.

### Speech Patterns

Felix's dialogue is consistently precise, economical, and weaponised:

- **Ch 1:** "You look like you could use one of these." / "A man with no plan. KL is a good city for men with no plans." The repetition technique (echoing Connor's language back at him) is established here and works perfectly. Felix validates by mirroring.
- **Ch 5:** "She'll leave though. They always do, the smart ones. They have somewhere to go back to." This is the most revealing Felix line in the sample. It is a knife disguised as sympathy. It isolates Connor from Priya (the warning) while appearing to empathise. And the rhythm is right -- short, declarative, the German precision sharpened to a point.
- **Ch 5:** "The system is designed to make you go home." The pitch. This passage is described as "polished, rehearsed -- a script he'd delivered in hostels across Asia," which is the correct authorial framing. The narration tells us what Connor cannot see.
- **Ch 5:** "Nothing illegal. Just... logistics." The ellipsis is key. Felix does not say "nothing illegal" as a flat statement; he places a pause before "logistics," letting the word arrive with the weight of considered casualness. This is a man who has practiced making lies sound like afterthoughts.

### Controlled Menace

The one moment of Felix's mask slipping -- Ch 9, the phone shop window -- is handled with surgical precision:

"For one second -- too brief for analysis, too long for comfort -- Felix's face was not the face Connor knew. The warm smile was absent. The easy charm was absent. What remained was something Connor had never seen on Felix's features: flat, still, and cold."

This is the single most important Felix moment in Parts 1-2, and it lands. The key is that Connor sees it and immediately rationalises it away ("Everyone looks weird when they're caught off-guard"), which is both Connor's machine operating and Felix's design succeeding. The reader sees through Connor's denial while Connor cannot.

### Felix's Absence

In Chapters 13, 17, and 20, Felix is physically absent from scenes but present through text messages. This is structurally correct -- Felix recedes as the schedule takes over, the handler becoming a ghost in the machine. His texts are appropriately terse ("Big day tomorrow. Come find me" / "Need to see you. Rooftop. Now. Important.") -- the escalation from casual to imperative mirrors his tightening control.

### Minor Concern

Felix is described in Ch 1 as having been at The Lantern "a few weeks" but the Canon establishes him as having been there much longer. This was flagged in the continuity pass as a deferred issue. For voice purposes, the inconsistency is minor -- it could be read as Felix understating his stay, which would be in character.

## 3. Priya Sharma's Voice

### Overall Assessment: GOOD (7.5/10)

Priya is the most difficult voice in the ensemble because she must be intelligent enough to see through everything, sympathetic enough that her warnings land, and human enough that her inability to save Connor feels tragic rather than frustrating.

### Speech Patterns

- **Ch 5:** "We're not on an island." / "It's a state of mind, Priya." This exchange is efficient and establishes the dynamic: Priya is literal where Connor is performative. Her dialogue is shorter, sharper, more direct.
- **Ch 5:** "Are you learning things? Growing? Or are you just... in a different place, doing the same nothing?" -- The ellipsis before "in a different place" is Priya's equivalent of Felix's calculated pause, but where Felix pauses to manipulate, Priya pauses because the truth is difficult to deliver. The line itself is devastating and authentic to a British-educated medical student.
- **Ch 20:** "You stop. Right now. Today. No more deliveries. No more packages. No more Felix." -- The protocol voice. This is Priya in triage mode, and the medical-student training coming through in crisis is both canon-accurate and effective.
- **Ch 20:** "You promise. [...] Connor, if you go to that rooftop, you won't call Ahmad. You know you won't. The machine will run. Felix will smile." -- Note that Priya uses Connor's own metaphor ("the machine") here. This is a significant moment: she has observed him well enough to name his pathology in his own language. Whether this is intentional or accidental needs a decision -- if intentional, it should be set up earlier (Priya overhearing or Connor explaining the machine to her). If accidental, it still works as her being perceptive enough to identify the pattern independently, but the word "machine" specifically may feel too coincidental.

### Jade Bracelet Detail

The jade bracelet appears in:
- **Ch 9 (purchase):** "The jade was cool against her dark skin, vivid green against brown." Established with sensory precision.
- **Ch 13:** Not mentioned (Priya is not in this chapter).
- **Ch 20:** "the jade bracelet -- the one she'd bought at the Central Market on her second day in KL, the one she wore on her left wrist because her mother had told her jade should sit over the pulse -- was cool against his collarbone."

**Continuity error:** In Ch 9, Connor buys the bracelet for Priya at the Petaling Street night market. In Ch 20, the narrative says it's "the one she'd bought at the Central Market on her second day in KL." These are contradictory. The Ch 9 version (Connor buying it) is the canon event. Ch 20 needs correction.

### British-Indian Voice

Priya's Britishness comes through in vocabulary ("trainers" instead of sneakers, Ch 5) and in her precision of speech. Her Indian heritage appears primarily through:
- Her parents' details (cardiologist father, GP mother, Ealing)
- The Batu Caves scene (Ch 5), where she knows the Hindu context
- The jade bracelet's cultural significance (her mother's instruction)

Her code-switching to Gujarati (mentioned in the Canon) is not demonstrated in any of the six sampled chapters. At minimum, one phone call with her mother where she switches languages would ground this aspect of her identity.

### Voice Break: One Instance

**Ch 20, lines 119-120:** Priya is described as sitting "in the armchair and watched him go and felt the crack between them widen into something that could no longer be called a crack because cracks implied a surface that was still intact."

This extended metaphor is in Priya's POV section, but it reads more like the narrator than like Priya's interiority. A medical student's internal language for this moment would more likely be clinical: a fracture, a separation, a wound that won't close. The "crack/surface" abstraction is literary rather than character-grounded.

**Suggested fix:** Reframe in medical terms: "...felt the fracture between them widen into something she recognised from her orthopaedics rotation -- not a stress fracture anymore but a complete break, the kind where the two halves could not be held together by anything short of surgery, and Connor would not consent to surgery."

## 4. Narrative Tone

### Overall Assessment: STRONG (8.5/10)

The narrative maintains a consistent third-person-limited rotating POV with a darkly comic sensibility throughout. The tonal balance shifts appropriately from chapter to chapter:

| Chapter | Comedy | Horror | Satire | Notes |
|---------|--------|--------|--------|-------|
| Ch 1 | 40% | 15% | 45% | Heavy satire of backpacker culture. Comedy in Connor's obliviousness. Horror only in dramatic irony (final paragraphs). |
| Ch 5 | 30% | 20% | 50% | Peak satire (the content-creation sequence). Horror emerges in Felix's pitch. |
| Ch 9 | 25% | 30% | 45% | Death map as "content" is peak satirical horror. Razak's history tilts toward genuine dread. |
| Ch 13 | 15% | 50% | 35% | Horror dominates. David's story is the novel's moral centre. Comedy confined to Jake's keg stand. |
| Ch 17 | 10% | 45% | 45% | The montage structure produces a flattened affect that is itself horrifying. Megan's email is the emotional anchor. |
| Ch 20 | 5% | 70% | 25% | Horror ascendant. Wei's back room is pure dread. Almost no comedy. |

The tonal arc from Ch 1 (satirical comedy) to Ch 20 (near-pure horror) is exactly right. The comedy recedes as the danger becomes undeniable. The satire persists longer than the comedy, because the satire is structural (the Instagram captions, the bank balance, the absurdity of Connor's position) rather than tonal (jokes, timing, punchlines).

### Darkly Comic Peaks

The best dark comedy moments in the sample:

1. **Ch 1:** Connor missing the DEATH FOR DRUG TRAFFICKERS sign while reading his mum's Instagram comment, the backlight colouring her message "the shade of arterial blood." Timing, image, and irony working in perfect concert.
2. **Ch 5:** The LinkedIn post that trails off into nothing. "He didn't know what you needed." The silence after the abandoned sentence is the joke, and it is brutal.
3. **Ch 9:** "Uncle, this is sick." / "Can I photograph the map?" Connor treating a death archive as content. Razak's expression: "something between pity and disgust, with recognition underneath." The comedy is in the gap between what Connor sees (content) and what exists (sixty-seven dead people).
4. **Ch 9:** Jake negotiating the watch price UP from 80 to 100 ringgit. Pure comic relief, perfectly timed (between the death-map scene and the phone-shop-window scene), functioning as the breath before the next plunge.
5. **Ch 13:** Jake's keg stand attempt during David's story. The juxtaposition is the joke -- mortal dread at one end of the bar, acrobatic drinking at the other.
6. **Ch 17:** Connor watching a Marvel film and finding comfort in "a story in which the rules were clear and the consequences were just and nobody died who didn't deserve to." The irony is exquisite because Connor is in a story where the rules are clear (Malaysian drug law) and the consequences are just (he is trafficking drugs) and he will die.

### Tonal Concern

**Ch 17, Day 30 section:** The description of Danny as occupying "the disposable middle" of the chain, and the karaoke bar passage, shifts briefly into a more hard-boiled crime register: "Connor did not ask why." This sentence, isolated on its own line, reads like noir. The noir register is not wrong -- it is one of the novel's secondary voices -- but it appears abruptly here and then vanishes. If the noir voice is going to appear, it should be seeded earlier (perhaps in the Ch 13 bar scene) so it does not feel like a tonal intrusion.

## 6. Sensory Details (KL-Specific)

### Overall Assessment: EXCELLENT (9/10)

The sensory environment is one of the novel's great achievements. KL is not a backdrop but a body -- it sweats, smells, breathes, and roars.

### Heat/Humidity

Present in every chapter, with well-varied descriptions:
- Ch 1: "walking into someone's open mouth"
- Ch 5: "the lunchtime heat, which was a statement of intent"
- Ch 9: Implied (night-market section, "warm and chaotic")
- Ch 13: "a warmth different from the air's warmth, more internal, more generous"
- Ch 17: "the midday sun was a white disc in a white sky, the heat landing on exposed skin like a physical weight"
- Ch 20: "The morning heat of Petaling Street hit him"

No two descriptions use the same metaphor. This is disciplined and effective.

### Food

The food writing is exceptional and specific:
- Char kway teow (Ch 1, Ch 9)
- Nasi lemak (Ch 5, Ch 9, Ch 17, Ch 20 -- Mama Siti's, always)
- Roti canai (Ch 5, Ch 17)
- Teh tarik (Ch 9 -- the pulled-tea passage is a highlight, with Razak's theatrical pour and the anecdote about Ah Beng)
- Satay (Ch 9 -- "peanut sauce thick enough to stand a skewer in")
- Korean food (Ch 17 -- spicy tofu stew, placed correctly in Bukit Bintang)
- Banana leaf rice (Ch 17 -- accurately described, including the folding protocol)

The food functions as both sensory detail and cultural texture. Connor's relationship to food shifts from consumption-for-content (Ch 1, Ch 5) to consumption-for-comfort (Ch 13, Ch 17), tracking his psychological state.

### Call to Prayer

- Ch 1: "the call to prayer from Masjid Jamek, distant but clear, a human voice rising above the traffic noise"
- Ch 9: "the call to prayer rose from Masjid Jamek, long and wavering, reaching for something above the rooftops"
- Ch 13: "Morning came the way KL mornings came: with heat and noise and the call to prayer"

Consistent. Always placed at dawn or dusk. Always from Masjid Jamek. Functions as a structural marker and as a metaphor for the warnings Connor ignores (the call asks "Are you awake? Are you listening?" in Ch 9).

### Petaling Street Smells

The olfactory palette is layered and consistent:
- Incense from the temple (Ch 1, Ch 9, Ch 13, Ch 20)
- Char kway teow smoke (Ch 1, Ch 9)
- Diesel exhaust (Ch 1, Ch 20)
- Sulfurous storm drains (Ch 1)
- Tiger Balm (Ch 1)
- Damp wood (Ch 1)

The only concern is that the sulfurous drain smell, vividly established in Ch 1, does not reappear in the later chapters. A brief recurrence in one of the Part 2 night scenes would reinforce the olfactory continuity.

## 7. Dark Comedy Effectiveness

### Overall Assessment: STRONG (8/10)

The dark comedy works because it arises organically from character rather than being imposed by narration. Connor is funny because he is oblivious, and the comedy is in the gap between what he sees and what exists. The novel never tells a joke -- it presents Connor's perception alongside reality and lets the reader supply the horror.

### Timing

The comedy-before-horror pattern is consistently executed:
- **Ch 1:** Comedic airport sequence (filming, slang, missed sign) --> horror of the final paragraph ("the city where he would die")
- **Ch 5:** LinkedIn comedy --> Felix's pitch (menace)
- **Ch 9:** "This is sick" / Jake's watch --> phone shop window (dread)
- **Ch 13:** Jake's Rolex story / keg stand --> David's testimony (horror)
- **Ch 17:** Marvel film comfort --> Megan's email (emotional horror)
- **Ch 20:** No comedy preceding the horror. This is correct -- by Ch 20, the comedy tank is empty and the novel should feel it.

### One Weakness

The Jake material, while consistently funny, risks becoming one-note. In the sampled chapters, Jake's function is exclusively comic relief -- the keg stand, the watch, the TikTok, the "dude." His canon role as "The Mirror" (a dumber Connor who survives by luck) requires at least one moment in Parts 1-2 where his obliviousness mirrors Connor's in a way that is unsettling rather than funny. The watch negotiation scene in Ch 9 comes closest but plays it purely for laughs. Consider whether one of Jake's Part 2 appearances should include a beat where Connor recognises himself in Jake and flinches.

## 8. Specific Line-Level Fixes

### Fix 1: Jade Bracelet Origin (Ch 20)

**Problem line (Ch 20, approx. line 125):**
> "the jade bracelet -- the one she'd bought at the Central Market on her second day in KL, the one she wore on her left wrist because her mother had told her jade should sit over the pulse"

**Issue:** Ch 9 clearly establishes that Connor bought Priya the jade bracelet at the Petaling Street night market. The "Central Market" / "second day" / "she'd bought" is a continuity error that also undermines the Ch 9 scene's emotional weight (Connor spending Felix's money on a gift for Priya).

**Suggested fix:**
> "the jade bracelet -- the one Connor had bought her at the Petaling Street night market, the one she wore on her left wrist because her mother had told her jade should sit over the pulse"

### Fix 2: Identical Closing Lines (Ch 13 / Ch 17)

**Problem:** Both chapters end with the phrase "the name in the bed frame waited, patient and carved and true."

**Issue:** Verbatim repetition across two chapter endings reads as an oversight, not a refrain.

**Suggested fix for Ch 17 (the later usage):**
> "the ceiling fan turning in the dorm where a name waited in the wood, the same name that had waited for the last one, the same patience, the same silence."

This preserves the LENA motif while varying the language and adding the "last one" echo (connecting to Lena's story).

### Fix 3: Narrator Voice Intrusion (Ch 20)

**Problem line (Ch 20, approx. line 121):**
> "the man who had been carrying packages would become the man who had carried packages, past tense, the tense of consequences"

**Suggested fix:**
> "the man who had been carrying packages would become the man who had carried packages, and the difference between the two was everything"

### Fix 4: Priya's "Machine" Reference (Ch 20)

**Problem line (Ch 20, approx. line 175):**
> "The machine will run. Felix will smile."

**Issue:** Priya using the word "machine" to describe Connor's rationalisation pattern is powerful but unearned -- there is no scene in the sampled chapters where Connor uses this word aloud or where Priya would have encountered it. In a third-person-limited POV rotating between characters, Priya's use of Connor's private metaphor breaks the POV unless it has been established that he has shared it with her.

**Suggested fixes (choose one):**
- **Option A:** Add a brief scene in an earlier chapter (Ch 14 or Ch 16) where Connor, in a moment of self-awareness, says something like "There's this thing my brain does -- it's like a machine, it just... explains everything away." This gives Priya access to the word.
- **Option B:** Change the line to: "You'll talk yourself out of it. You always do. Felix will smile, and you'll find a reason, and tomorrow you'll be carrying another package." This preserves Priya's insight without borrowing Connor's metaphor.

Option B is cleaner and requires no retroactive changes.

### Fix 5: Missing Catchphrases

**Issue:** "Relax, it's Asia" and "That's what travel insurance is for" are listed as Connor's defining catchphrases in VICTIM_PROFILE.md but do not appear in the sampled chapters.

**Suggested insertions:**
- "Relax, it's Asia" -- Insert in Ch 5, during the conversation with Priya about his lifestyle ("Maybe it is," Priya said. Not unkindly. / Connor shrugged. "Relax, it's Asia. Everything works out here."). This would be in character and would sharpen the irony.
- "That's what travel insurance is for" -- Insert in Ch 13, as part of Connor's mental rationalisation after David's story, or as a dismissive comment to Jake on the walk home. This would function as dark comedy.

### Fix 6: "She'll Be Right" Absence in Part 2

**Issue:** Connor's signature phrase disappears entirely from the Part 2 samples. By this point in the story, the phrase should be appearing in increasingly hollow, desperate contexts.

**Suggested insertion:** Ch 17, rest-day section (Day 27), internal monologue: "He thought about the money. RM12,180. *She'll be right.* The phrase arrived as it always did -- automatic, reflexive, the Australian mantra that had carried him through twelve countries and was beginning to sound, even to him, like something a man says when he is falling and cannot reach the railing."

This preserves the phrase, shows its degradation, and connects to the machine metaphor without being heavy-handed.

### Fix 7: Building-Breathes Motif Gap in Part 2

**Issue:** The hostel-as-living-entity motif disappears after Ch 13 in the sampled chapters.

**Suggested insertion:** Ch 17, rest-day section or closing passage: "He lay on Bed 7 and felt the building settle around him -- the pipes groaning, the wood contracting in the heat, the slow respiration of a structure that had been breathing since 1928 and would continue breathing long after the man on the mattress stopped."

## 9. Summary of Findings

### Strengths
1. **Connor's voice** is remarkably consistent -- the slang, the self-deception, the breezy internal narration that gradually curdles into panic.
2. **Felix's voice** achieves the nearly impossible: charm that the reader can feel AND see through simultaneously.
3. **The machine metaphor** is the novel's best structural device, introduced cleanly, escalated carefully, and given a satisfying near-failure.
4. **KL sensory detail** is exceptional -- the city is rendered as a living body with extraordinary olfactory and thermal precision.
5. **Dark comedy timing** (comic relief before horror beats) is almost flawless.
6. **The tonal arc** from satirical comedy (Ch 1) to near-pure horror (Ch 20) is controlled and organic.

### Weaknesses
1. **Priya's POV** occasionally drifts into essayistic narrator-voice rather than her own medical/analytical interiority.
2. **Priya's use of "the machine"** in Ch 20 is a POV break that needs resolution.
3. **Jake's voice** is consistent but risks one-dimensionality; his Mirror function from the Canon is underserved in the sample.
