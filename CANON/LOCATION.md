# Location Profile

## The Hostel

**Name:** The Lantern Hostel (Rumah Tanglung)
**Type:** Backpacker / Heritage
**Star Rating:** 3 (Hostelworld: 7.8/10, 340 reviews)
**Price Range:** RM 35-85 per night ($8-20 USD)

### History
Built in 1928 as a shophouse by a Hokkien merchant family. Served as a textile warehouse in the 1930s, was requisitioned by the Japanese military during WWII occupation (1942-1945) and used as an administrative office (locals whisper "interrogation center"), became a gambling den in the 1960s, was briefly a brothel in the 1970s, sat abandoned through the 1980s and 1990s, and was converted into a backpacker hostel in 2009 by its current owner, Razak bin Omar. Every iteration left its mark. The building doesn't forget its uses.

### Architecture
- **Style:** Pre-war Straits Chinese shophouse - narrow, deep, three stories, with a central air well. Ornate plasterwork on the facade (chipped, faded), original tile floors (cracked, uneven), timber shutters on upper windows. The air well lets in light and rain to the center of the building.
- **Floors:** 3 (plus a rooftop terrace added in 2012, illegally)
- **Total beds:** 42 (6 dorm rooms of varying sizes, 2 private rooms)
- **Notable features:** The air well in the center of the building creates an internal courtyard effect. Red paper lanterns hang from every ceiling, giving the place its name and a perpetual warm glow. Original WWII-era Japanese signage is still visible under peeling paint on the second-floor landing. The staircase is so narrow two people cannot pass. The rooftop has a view of the Petronas Towers.

### The Death Location (Specific)
Connor dies in a holding cell at the Dang Wangi District Police Headquarters, approximately 2 kilometers from the hostel. The cell is on the ground floor, end of a corridor, 3m x 3m, concrete walls painted grey, a steel bench bolted to the wall, bars on the front painted institutional green, a squat toilet in the corner, a single fluorescent tube light that never turns off. The cell number is C-7.

## The City

**City:** Kuala Lumpur (KL)
**Country:** Malaysia
**Population:** ~8 million (metro area)
**Known for:** Petronas Twin Towers, street food, cultural diversity (Malay, Chinese, Indian, international), shopping, nightlife, as a Southeast Asian travel hub

### Geography
- **Climate:** Tropical equatorial - hot and humid year-round, 27-33Â°C. Afternoon thunderstorms almost daily.
- **Terrain:** Urban, relatively flat, surrounded by hills. The city sits in a valley at the confluence of two rivers (Klang and Gombak).
- **Weather during story:** Late monsoon season. Heavy afternoon rains, oppressive humidity, occasional flooding in low-lying areas of Chinatown. The air is thick, wet, and smells of rain on hot asphalt.

### Culture
- **Language(s):** Bahasa Melayu (national), English (widely spoken, especially in tourist areas), Mandarin, Tamil, Cantonese, Hokkien
- **Religion(s):** Islam (official), Buddhism, Hinduism, Christianity, Taoism
- **Customs relevant to story:** Malaysia's dual legal system (civil law and Sharia law for Muslims). The strict drug laws that carry mandatory death penalty. The cultural emphasis on respect for authority that Connor completely lacks. The concept of "face" and how Connor's loud, brash behavior is perceived.

### Tourist Scene
- **Backpacker popularity:** High - KL is a major hub on the Southeast Asia backpacker trail
- **Type of tourists:** All types - budget backpackers cluster in Chinatown/Petaling Street, mid-range in Bukit Bintang, luxury around KLCC
- **Danger level:** Generally safe for tourists, but drug laws are severe and strictly enforced. Travel advisories warn about drug trafficking penalties.

## Sensory Environment

### Sounds
- Morning: The call to prayer from Masjid Jamek (5:30 AM), motorbike engines, metal shutters rolling up on shophouses, the clatter of Mama Siti's pans in the hostel kitchen, mynah birds
- Afternoon: Traffic noise on Petaling Street, the rumble of approaching thunderstorms, rain hammering on zinc roofs, market vendors calling out prices in Cantonese, the hiss of woks in hawker stalls
- Night: Bass from the clubs on Changkat Bukit Bintang, the buzz of neon signs, motorcycle taxis, drunk tourists, the clicking of mahjong tiles from an upstairs window, cats fighting in the alley behind the hostel
- Unique sounds: The particular creak of the hostel's wooden staircase (third step from top squeaks), the rattle of the ceiling fans, the air well amplifying rain into a roar

### Smells
- Hostel: Damp wood, Tiger Balm, cheap laundry detergent, the ghost of cigarette smoke, cooking oil from the kitchen, and always, beneath everything, the slightly sweet rot of an old building in the tropics
- Street: Char kway teow frying, durian from the fruit stalls, diesel exhaust, incense from the Guan Di Temple, wet concrete after rain, the sulfurous blast from open drains
- Specific to death location: Disinfectant, stale sweat, concrete dust, the metallic smell of the cell bars, and faintly, Rizal's cigarette smoke

### Textures
- Surfaces: The original encaustic tile floors of the hostel - cool, slightly uneven, some cracked. The damp wooden bunk frames. The thin hostel mattresses that feel like vinyl-covered foam. The sticky plastic chairs on the rooftop.
- Climate feel: The humidity is a physical presence - skin is always slightly damp, clothes never fully dry, everything feels tacky. Walking outside is like breathing through a warm, wet cloth. Air conditioning in malls and police stations hits like a wall of cold.

### Tastes
- Local food: Nasi lemak (coconut rice with sambal, anchovies, peanuts, egg), roti canai (flaky flatbread with dhal), teh tarik (pulled tea, sweet and milky), char kway teow (fried flat noodles), satay, and Mama Siti's version of all of the above
- Hostel offerings: Free breakfast (white bread toast, margarine, kaya jam, instant coffee). Mama Siti's nasi lemak (RM5, worth it). The communal kitchen is stocked with abandoned condiments from dozens of nations.

### Visual Details
- Colors: Red (lanterns, Chinese signage, temple decorations), gold (temple details, the warm glow inside the hostel), grey-green (the tropical damp on old walls), the blue-white of fluorescent lights in the police station
- Light quality: Inside the hostel, warm and amber from the lanterns and low-wattage bulbs. Outside, either the harsh tropical sun or the eerie neon glow of Petaling Street at night. The police station is bathed in cold fluorescent white.
- Distinctive visuals: The Petronas Towers visible from the rooftop, framed between shophouse roofs and tangles of electrical wire. The red lanterns of the hostel reflecting in puddles after rain. The narrow corridor of the hostel staircase, walls covered in stickers from travelers past.

## The Staff

### Reception
**Name:** Mei Ling Chen
**Description:** 24, Malaysian-Chinese, sharp-eyed, perpetually unimpressed. Studied hospitality at Taylor's University but took the hostel job because it was close to home. She sees everything, says little, and keeps a mental file on every guest. Wears her hair in a practical ponytail and a polo shirt with the hostel logo (a red lantern).
**Role in death:** Warning-giver. She tries to warn Connor twice - once subtly, once directly. Both times he doesn't listen. She is not complicit but she is unsurprised. She's seen this pattern before.

### Staff Knowledge
Mama Siti knows, in the way that old buildings know - through their bones. She speaks of Connor in the past tense before he dies. Uncle Razak knows about the trafficking network and has made an uneasy peace with it: they don't recruit from his hostel, and he doesn't report them. Connor's arrival disrupts this arrangement because Felix has broken the rule. Mei Ling suspects something is wrong but doesn't have the full picture. Aziz, the former cop, sees the signs but has learned that intervention only makes things worse.

## Hostel Atmosphere

### Day Vibe
Lazy, sociable. Travelers nursing hangovers, planning day trips, arguing about the best hawker stalls. The common room TV plays CNN or football. Mama Siti's cooking fills the ground floor with spice. The ceiling fans rotate slowly. It feels safe. It feels like a home away from home.

### Night Vibe
Shifts after 10 PM. The ground floor empties as guests head to Changkat or Petaling Street. The rooftop becomes a drinking spot. Conversations get louder, more confessional. Felix holds court. The hostel's red lanterns glow in the dark air well like something alive. The building feels older at night, more aware. The staircase creaks when no one is on it.

### Guest Demographics
20s-30s, predominantly Western (Australian, British, German, American, Scandinavian), with some Japanese, Korean, and Southeast Asian travelers. Mix of short-stay tourists (2-3 nights) and semi-permanent "digital nomads" (weeks to months). Everyone knows each other's names but not their surnames.

### Reputation
Hostelworld: "Great location, good vibes, the nasi lemak is amazing. Weird owner but in a charming way. Showers are lukewarm. Would stay again." One review from 8 months ago: "Something about this place feels off. Beautiful building though."

## Practical Details

### Getting There
Most backpackers arrive at KL Sentral station (train from airport) or at TBS (bus terminal, from other Malaysian cities). The hostel is a 10-minute walk from Pasar Seni LRT station, down Petaling Street, past the market stalls, turn left at the temple. The entrance is a narrow door between a phone repair shop and a traditional Chinese medicine store.

### Check-in Process
Ring the bell. Mei Ling appears. Passport photocopy. Cash payment preferred (but Grab Pay accepted). Sign the guest book - an actual paper ledger, which Uncle Razak insists on. Bed assignment. No keys for dorms. Lockers available (BYO padlock, or buy one for RM10). WiFi password changes weekly and is written on a whiteboard behind reception. House rules are posted in English, Malay, Mandarin, and Japanese.

### Rules
1. Quiet after 11 PM (never enforced)
2. No drugs on premises (enforced - this is Malaysia)
3. No outside food in dorms (ignored)
4. Check-out by 11 AM
5. Do not leave valuables unattended
6. **Written in red at the bottom: "Guests are responsible for knowing and obeying Malaysian law. The hostel is not liable."**
